# Appearance Flexible Job Validation V1

## Purpose

Validate `Appearance-flexible jobs` as an information, listing, and advertising market.

This is not a job board build plan.

This is not an employment placement plan.

Direct job matching, placement success fees, and acting as a recruiter are excluded at this phase.

## Definition

Appearance-flexible jobs are jobs where employers clearly accept one or more of:

- tattoo OK
- visible tattoo OK
- covered tattoo OK
- piercing OK
- hair color OK
- nail OK
- beard OK
- no-customer-facing role
- uniform covers appearance
- creative / street / alternative style accepted

## Executive Judgment

The market is worth validating.

The core value is not:

```text
more job listings
```

The core value is:

```text
reducing uncertainty around appearance restrictions before applying.
```

The best first product is not a job site.

It is:

```text
verified appearance-flexibility information + employer demand validation
```

## 1. Job Seeker Pain

## Pain Summary

Job seekers with tattoos, piercings, bright hair, nails, or non-standard style face a search problem.

They do not only ask:

```text
Is there a job?
```

They ask:

```text
Will I be rejected because of how I look?
```

## Specific Pain Points

| Pain | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Tattoo policy is not shown | User cannot judge before applying |
| Visible vs covered tattoo is unclear | Major difference in acceptance |
| Hand/neck/face tattoos are treated differently | General "tattoo OK" is not enough |
| Interview rejection risk | Wastes time and creates shame |
| Asking directly feels awkward | Sensitive condition |
| Customer-facing rules differ | Store/office roles vary by brand |
| Industry norms are unclear | Apparel, beauty, warehouse, construction differ |
| Foreign workers may misunderstand norms | Japan-specific uncertainty |
| Night-work search feels risky | Some users want appearance-flexible jobs outside night-work |
| Existing filters are incomplete | General job sites rarely handle nuance |

## Pain Strength

```text
High for affected users
Medium as a mass market
```

This is not universal, but for affected users the pain is sharp.

## Job Seeker Validation Questions

Ask:

1. Have you avoided applying because tattoo/piercing/hair rules were unclear?
2. Have you been rejected or warned because of appearance?
3. Which condition matters most: tattoo, piercing, hair color, nails, beard, clothing?
4. Is covered tattoo OK enough, or do you need visible tattoo OK?
5. Which jobs do you search for?
6. Would a verified list save time?
7. Would you share anonymous appearance preferences to find jobs?
8. Would you pay for premium search/support, or should employers pay?

## 2. Employer Pain

## Pain Summary

Employers do not usually wake up wanting a "tattoo OK listing."

They want:

```text
more applicants who fit the role and will not create appearance-policy conflict.
```

## Employer Pain Points

| Pain | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Labor shortage | More acceptable applicants matter |
| Applicants self-reject | Some candidates assume they are not accepted |
| Awkward interview mismatch | Employer discovers appearance conflict late |
| Brand concern | Employers fear publishing permissive rules broadly |
| Staff rules are inconsistent | Store managers may differ |
| Role-specific acceptance | Back-office/warehouse may be OK, front desk may not |
| Existing job boards lack nuance | Hard to communicate conditions precisely |
| Younger applicants value flexibility | Differentiation advantage |

## Employer Pain Strength

```text
Medium
```

The pain is strongest in roles with:

- labor shortage
- high turnover
- youth/creative workers
- non-customer-facing work
- fashion/beauty/street culture
- foreign staff
- late shift or flexible shift work

## Employer Validation Questions

Ask:

1. Do you allow tattoos, piercings, hair color, nails, or beards?
2. Are rules different by role?
3. Are visible tattoos acceptable?
4. Would you publicly state this in a job listing?
5. Do applicants ask about appearance rules?
6. Have appearance mismatches wasted interview time?
7. Would clear appearance filters improve applicant quality?
8. Would you pay for a listing that reaches appearance-flexible job seekers?

## 3. Value Of Showing "Tattoo OK"

## Why The Label Has Value

The phrase:

```text
Tattoo OK
```

reduces uncertainty immediately.

But it is too vague alone.

Better labels:

- tattoo OK if covered
- visible tattoo OK
- hand/neck tattoo not accepted
- small tattoo OK
- piercing OK
- hair color OK
- nails OK
- no-customer-facing role
- uniform covers tattoo
- depends on role

## Value To Job Seekers

It saves:

- search time
- emotional friction
- awkward questions
- failed interviews
- wasted applications

## Value To Employers

It can:

- increase applications from self-excluded candidates
- reduce mismatch
- show workplace openness
- attract creative/youth workers
- differentiate from conservative employers

## Risk To Employers

Some employers may fear:

- brand damage
- unwanted applicant pool
- internal policy exposure
- customer perception
- ambiguity if rules change by manager

Therefore, the label must allow nuance.

## Recommended Label System

Use structured labels:

| Label | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Tattoo OK: Covered only | Tattoos allowed if hidden during work |
| Tattoo OK: Visible small | Small visible tattoos accepted |
| Tattoo OK: Visible broad | Visible tattoos generally accepted |
| Piercing OK | Piercings accepted within role rules |
| Hair color OK | Non-natural colors accepted |
| Nail OK | Nail design/length accepted |
| No-customer-facing | Appearance rules are lighter |
| Role-dependent | Rules vary by position |
| Ask before applying | Employer is open but case-by-case |

## 4. Application Rate Improvement Potential

## Hypothesis

If appearance conditions are clearly shown, affected job seekers are more likely to apply.

Reason:

```text
uncertainty is a conversion blocker.
```

## Expected Impact

Potential increase is strongest where:

- role already struggles to hire
- applicants are style-conscious
- job is part-time/hourly
- application friction is low
- appearance policy is currently hidden
- employer is genuinely flexible

## How To Measure

Do not claim improvement before data.

Measure:

- listing view to application intent
- survey "would apply" rate
- employer-reported applicant volume
- number of saves/clicks on appearance-flex labels
- applicant questions before/after label
- mismatch/no-show rate if data becomes available

## Test Design

Create two versions of sample listings:

Version A:

```text
normal job listing
```

Version B:

```text
same listing + structured appearance-flex labels
```

Ask job seekers:

```text
Which would you apply to?
```

Target:

```text
100 responses
```

Success signal:

```text
30%+ of affected respondents say labels increase willingness to apply.
```

Strong signal:

```text
50%+ say they would apply to jobs they otherwise would skip.
```

## 5. Listing Fee Potential

## Early Listing Product

Do not charge for placement.

Charge for:

```text
appearance-flexible listing visibility
```

Possible packages:

| Package | Price | Contents |
|---|---:|---|
| Validation listing | 3,000-5,000 JPY | Listed in pilot page for 30 days |
| Verified appearance policy | 10,000 JPY | Employer survey + structured label |
| Sponsored pilot listing | 15,000-30,000 JPY | Top placement in niche page |
| Demand report sponsor | 30,000-50,000 JPY | Access to anonymous demand findings |

## Buyer Willingness Hypothesis

Employers may pay if:

- they are hiring urgently
- existing job boards are expensive
- they want younger/creative applicants
- they have roles where appearance flexibility is an advantage
- they believe applicants self-exclude today

## Weakness

Employer willingness is unproven.

Many may say:

```text
Interesting, but we do not want to pay yet.
```

This is why employer pre-sell is required.

## Listing Fee Validation Target

Interview:

```text
20 employers
```

Success signal:

```text
3 employers agree to paid pilot listing
```

Strong signal:

```text
5 employers pay 5,000-10,000 JPY for a validation listing
```

## 6. Advertising Model Potential

## Possible Advertising Buyers

Ads could come from:

- tattoo studios
- piercing studios
- apparel brands
- beauty salons
- street fashion brands
- hair color products
- nightlife-adjacent employers
- creative schools
- foreigner-friendly services
- job platforms

## Why Ads Could Work

The audience is identity-specific.

If the site attracts people searching for appearance-flexible work, it also attracts advertisers who want:

- tattooed users
- fashion/street culture users
- beauty/self-expression users
- foreigner-friendly audiences
- youth/part-time workers

## Weakness

Advertising usually requires traffic.

It is not the best first revenue model.

Better order:

1. employer paid validation listing
2. sponsored listing
3. demand report
4. ads after traffic exists

## Ad Model Judgment

```text
CAUTION
```

Possible later, not first revenue.

## Legal / Scope Boundary

Allowed in validation:

- listing information
- employer policy survey
- advertising
- sponsored listings
- anonymous market report
- link out to employer application pages

Avoid:

- selecting candidates for employers
- arranging interviews
- recommending specific candidate to employer
- taking success fee for hire
- handling employment contract formation

If moving toward matching or hire-based fees:

```text
legal review required first
```

## Build vs Buy

Existing job platforms can copy the filter.

Defensibility must come from:

- trusted appearance policy taxonomy
- verified employer answers
- job seeker community
- niche SEO around sensitive conditions
- employer education
- anonymous demand data
- brand as "appearance-flexible work" authority

Without verification/community, large job sites can copy.
